On Google, CS and web development

Tweet
Fri 03 January 2014
By Daniel Roseman

In Technical.

tags: python programming google

So, I am leaving Google.

It's not entirely my decision, but neither am I particularly bitter about it. I joined almost three years ago as a web developer for the "people development" department, rather than in the normal engineering organisation, and was told from the start by the recruiter that I'd find it very difficult to transition to a proper engineering role because I don't have a computer science background.

In the intervening time, I've expended quite a lot of effort in trying to make that transition happen: most recently by actually re-interviewing internally for the "software engineering" ladder. Despite a large amount of preparation, when it came to the real interviews I bombed: so now, since my current role is going away and I haven't been able to move to the proper SWE one, and despite three years of positive feedback and ratings, I'm out. (Joining Government Digital Services, since you ask, who seem to be happy to have me.)

As I say, I'm not particularly bitter; all this is just scene-setting for some thoughts on the necessity of computer science knowledge in web development jobs.

As I hinted above, I'm an entirely self-taught programmer. I have no computing qualifications at all; my undergraduate degree, believe it or not, is in European Studies, and my postgraduate diploma is in newspaper journalism. I came into professional software development via a very circuitous route that involved product management, charity work, redundancy, freelancing and a very large amount of luck, and I'm eternally grateful that certain folk seem to have taken me seriously in that role despite my odd background. (Everyone, that is, apart from Google. Hey ho.)

For the first few years of my career, I didn't feel that I was missing anything at all from my lack of CS knowledge. I was missing software development knowledge - version control, release management, estimation, etc - but that came quickly as part of working with a team. As far as programming was concerned, I'd been doing that since I was ten (which is more years ago than I care to admit) and while there are always new things to learn, I was pretty happy with how my knowledge and experience stacked up against everyone else I was working with. And, again, they were pretty happy with how I was working, too.

So it wasn't until I had to start studying for the transfer interviews at Google that I spent any time learning the kinds of things I would have had to learn on a CS course: in particular, algorithms, data structures and compilers. I followed several courses on Coursera: the two parts of Tim Roughgarden's algorithms course, and Alex Aiken's compilers one (and I even completed some of them). I read several algorithms books, and did all sorts of sample questions about graph traversal on Boggle boards and remote control keypads.

(As an aside, my general problem with algorithms courses has been the amount of maths involved. I don't find I have any natural facility with maths, so even though I understand the concepts I struggle to apply them in a theoretical situation. It is possible to study algorithms without maths, and some books take that approach - ie leaving out or allowing you to skip things like proving the complexity of an algorithm - but most of the courses I tried had maths as an integral part, which made it difficult for me.)

So, despite my failure at the Google transfer, has it made me a better programmer? Yes, without question. It really is good to have an understanding of what happens when you call sort() on a list, and know exactly why Python's dictionaries have a constant-time lookup and how that is achieved. And it's especially good to understand exactly what happens when you call a function, why that might be expensive, and how you can run out of stack space.

But, there's another question: has it made me a better web developer? Well: not really. I certainly have a better understanding of sorting algorithms

While it's nice to be able to point to a bit of code and say if it's O(n) or O(n^2), to be honest I knew the difference anyway, just without the technical terms.

And here's the crunch: for the type of work I do, this knowledge is esoteric at best. I enjoy diving into complex programming problems, but in my work they're mostly of the kind "How can I make this query in the most efficient way", and "How can I get these separate systems to talk together properly". The answer to those questions might involve graph or tree traversal; but more likely they don't.

I'm never going to be the guy who invents the next MapReduce, or who implements a better ranking algorithm for search, or who perfects voice recognition. There's been an intern sitting next to me for the past few months who's been doing amazing things with facial recognition: I'm never going to be able to do what he does.

But I am actually quite good at what I do do. As I mentioned, even at Google I've had nothing but positive feedback and above-average ratings. My colleagues here and in previous companies have relied on me to produce good designs, well-written and fully-tested code, and helpful feedback on their own code, and I've always delivered. But that, apparently, is not what Google want. They don't want someone who can actually do a job, as I have proved; they want someone who can theoretically do a job, which I can't.

Well, that's their problem; as I say, I really am not bitter. (OK, maybe just a bit.) It is just a same that some companies - and Google's certainly not the only one - are so focused on recruiting "engineering generalists" that they ignore the fact that software engineering is a very wide discipline, and it's possible to have good skills in one corner of it without necessarily having that theoretical underpinning. Or, as it came down to in my case, not knowing about Bloom filters.

In fact they do have a separate job ladder for "web developer", but that is very much focused on front-end development. My front-end skills are fine, and I enjoying doing that sort of thing sometimes, but it's not really where my interest lies. I've always been more focused on the back-end, but at Google you aren't supposed to do that unless you really are a "SWE".

And that's a shame: for me and other web devs who are excluded from working at Google, and indeed for the SWEs who do work on web stuff here but are forced to use the horribly over-engineered libraries that have been built for them to make web development more like "proper" Java programming - GWT and Closure, I'm looking at you. (And even though there's now big takeup of AngularJS, which is doing a lot to change that, the powers that be still do all they can to make working with it more like working with a statically-typed compiled language, greatly reducing its flexibility. Frustrating. I might have more to say on this in a future post.)
